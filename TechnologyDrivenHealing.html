Life Cycle Cost Guidelines Integrated with AS/NZS 4536: An Application Guide

AS/NZS 4536 is a comprehensive guide for applying life cycle costing (LCC) principles to manage the total cost of ownership (TCO) of assets. It provides a framework to assess costs over the asset's lifespan, including acquisition, operation, maintenance, and disposal costs. Here’s a step-by-step process flow with detailed descriptions integrated with our previously discussed guidelines.

Step 1: Define the Objective

Purpose: Clearly state the objective of the life cycle costing analysis.

Description: Determine the specific goals, such as minimizing total cost, comparing alternatives, or optimizing investment decisions.

Output: A well-defined objective statement outlining the expected outcomes of the LCC analysis.

Example Objectives:

  • Reduce maintenance costs.
  • Extend asset life.
  • Optimize energy usage.

Step 2: Establish the Scope

Purpose: Define the boundaries and extent of the analysis.

Description: Specify the asset or system under consideration, the time horizon, and the cost elements to be included.

Output: A scope document detailing the asset's boundaries, time frame, and cost elements.

Example Scope: Analysis of a fleet of vehicles over a 10-year period.

Step 3: Identify Cost Elements

Purpose: Catalog all relevant cost elements over the asset's life cycle.

Description: Identify acquisition costs (purchase, installation), operational costs (energy, labor), maintenance costs (planned and unplanned), and disposal costs (decommissioning, recycling).

Output: A comprehensive list of all cost elements associated with the asset.

What is Included? When calculating the whole-of-life cost of a facility, WLC should include:

  • Up-front Acquisition Costs: Initial costs of obtaining the facility, such as purchase price, design, planning, freight, installation, and initial training.
  • Lifetime Operating Costs: Costs incurred during the facility's life, including energy consumption, quality and safety, distribution, logistics, supplier staff wages, transport, program materials, and automatic cost of living increases.
  • Lifetime Maintenance and Support Costs: Costs to maintain the facility's dependability, such as administration, consumables, spare parts, minor repairs, and refresher training for staff.
  • Disposal Costs: Costs of removing or disposing of the facility after its economic life has ended, including transfer of ownership, trade-in, auction, or recycling.

What is Excluded? WLC should exclude:

  • Depreciation: An accounting allocation of capital costs over the life cycle, as including it would double count capital costs.
  • Capital Charge: The opportunity cost of capital, accounted for in the discount rate used in present value calculations.
  • Corporate Overheads: Costs that will be incurred regardless of the procurement or offer selected.
  • FTE Costs of Existing Staff: Costs of existing staff unless they are engaged directly due to the procurement.

Step 4: Gather Data

Purpose: Collect necessary data for cost estimation.

Description: Obtain historical data, manufacturer’s information, and expert estimates. Consider inflation rates, discount factors, and other financial parameters.

Output: A dataset comprising cost estimates, historical data, and financial parameters.

Example Data Sources:

  • Historical cost records.
  • Supplier quotes.
  • Industry benchmarks.

Step 5: Develop a Cost Model

Purpose: Create a model to calculate life cycle costs.

Description: Utilize software tools or spreadsheet models to input data and calculate costs over the asset's lifespan. The model should be flexible to allow scenario analysis.

Output: A functional cost model ready for data input and scenario testing.

Example Tools:

  • Specialized LCC software.
  • Excel spreadsheets with cost calculation formulas.

Step 6: Perform Cost Analysis

Purpose: Analyze the costs using the developed model.

Description: Input the collected data into the model. Perform calculations to determine the total life cycle cost. Conduct sensitivity analysis to understand the impact of varying assumptions.

Output: Detailed cost analysis reports and sensitivity analysis results.

Example Analysis:

  • Total cost of ownership calculations.
  • Net Present Value (NPV) analysis.

Step 7: Evaluate Alternatives

Purpose: Compare different options or scenarios.

Description: Use the cost model to evaluate different asset alternatives or operational scenarios. Compare the life cycle costs of each option to identify the most cost-effective solution.

Output: Comparative analysis of alternatives with recommendations for the best option.

Example Comparison: Cost analysis of two different types of machinery with varying efficiency and maintenance needs.

Step 8: Document Findings

Purpose: Compile and document the results of the LCC analysis.

Description: Prepare a comprehensive report detailing the objectives, scope, methodology, cost elements, data sources, analysis, and recommendations.

Output: A finalized life cycle costing report.

Example Report Sections:

  • Executive summary.
  • Methodology.
  • Detailed findings.
  • Recommendations.

Step 9: Review and Update

Purpose: Ensure the analysis remains relevant and accurate over time.

Description: Periodically review the assumptions, data, and model. Update the LCC analysis as necessary to reflect changes in costs, usage patterns, or economic conditions.

Output: Updated life cycle costing analysis reflecting current conditions.

Example Review Schedule: Annual updates to reflect changes in operational costs or asset performance.

Detailed Descriptions and Formulas

Step 1: Define the Objective

Establish clear, measurable goals for the LCC analysis. Examples include reducing maintenance costs, extending asset life, and optimizing energy usage.

Step 2: Establish the Scope

Define what the analysis will cover, including specific assets, subsystems, and time frames. For instance, an analysis of a fleet of vehicles over a 10-year period.

Step 3: Identify Cost Elements

Break down costs into categories:

  • Initial Acquisition Costs: Purchase price, design, planning, freight, installation, and initial training.
  • Lifetime Operating Costs: Energy consumption, quality and safety, distribution, logistics, supplier staff wages, transport, program materials, and automatic cost of living increases.
  • Lifetime Maintenance and Support Costs: Supplier administration costs, consumables, spare parts, minor repairs, labor, and refresher training for staff.
  • Disposal Costs: Transfer of ownership, trade-in, auction, or recycling.

Step 4: Gather Data

Collect accurate and relevant data from various sources like historical cost records, supplier quotes, and industry benchmarks.

Step 5: Develop a Cost Model

Choose an appropriate modeling tool or software, such as specialized LCC software or Excel spreadsheets with cost calculation formulas.

Step 6: Perform Cost Analysis

Input data into the model and run simulations, such as total cost of ownership calculations and NPV analysis.

Formula Example: LCC=(AC−TD)+(OC+RC)−RV\text{LCC} = (\text{AC} - \text{TD}) + (\text{OC} + \text{RC}) - \text{RV}LCC=(AC−TD)+(OC+RC)−RV

For refurbishment projects: LCC=(AC−TD)+DM+(OC+RC)−RV\text{LCC} = (\text{AC} - \text{TD}) + \text{DM} + (\text{OC} + \text{RC}) - \text{RV}LCC=(AC−TD)+DM+(OC+RC)−RV

Where:

  • AC: Initial Asset Acquisition / Capital Cost.
  • TD: Tax Depreciation Entitlements.
  • OC: Operating and Maintenance Costs.
  • RC: Replacement / Disposal / Upgrade Costs.
  • RV: Residual / Salvage Value.
  • DM: Deferred Maintenance.

Example Calculation: For a new facility with an initial cost (AC) of $5 million, tax depreciation (TD) of $500,000, operating and maintenance costs (OC) of $2 million over 20 years, replacement costs (RC) of $1 million, and a residual value (RV) of $1 million: LCC = (5,000,000 - 500,000) + (2,000,000 + 1,000,000) - 1,000,000 = $6,500,000

Step 7: Evaluate Alternatives

Compare the life cycle costs of different asset choices or operational strategies. For example, analyze two different types of machinery with varying efficiency and maintenance needs.

Step 8: Document Findings

Present the analysis in a clear, structured report, including sections like executive summary, methodology, detailed findings, and recommendations.

Step 9: Review and Update

Regularly update the LCC analysis to reflect new data and conditions. For example, perform annual updates to account for changes in operational costs or asset performance.

By following these steps, you can systematically apply AS/NZS 4536 principles to achieve optimal asset management and cost efficiency over the entire life cycle of their assets. This comprehensive approach ensures sustainable and cost-effective management of sport and recreation facilities, helping facility managers make informed decisions, optimize resource allocation, and secure funding by demonstrating long-term cost benefits
